ASUN robustness: reproduction results

Re-running Baker et al.’s own evaluation harness shows their published ASUN canonicalization is not, in fact, invariant to rotation and translation — ours is.

1. What we ran

Dataset: QM9 — 130,831 small organic molecules (elements H, C, N, O, F).

We reproduced Baker et al. 2024’s own evaluation harness (their examples/unique_canonical_representation.ipynb) verbatim. For each molecule: compute a baseline canonical pose, then apply a perturbation — a random rotation plus a random translation of the same molecule (a Haar-random rotation composed with an np.random.rand(3) shift). There is no coordinate noise: atoms are not jittered, only the rigid frame (rotation + translation) changes.

Metric: the paper’s own geometric Earth Mover Distance — POT’s ot.emd2 with uniform weights and Euclidean cost — between the baseline canonical pose and the re-canonicalized perturbed pose. EMD is permutation-invariant, so atom reordering introduced by a canonicalization does not affect the metric.

Point groups are labeled with the pointgroup library. Seed 42 throughout.

Why this test is decisive A canonicalization is by definition a function \(c\) with \(c(g\cdot x)=c(x)\) for every rigid transform \(g\) (rotation + translation) in the molecule’s orbit. With zero coordinate noise, an exact, numerically stable canonicalization has no way to produce anything other than EMD \(=0\) between the baseline pose and the re-canonicalized perturbed pose. This page is about whether that holds in practice.

5. Significance

What "EMD = 0" does and does not mean A canonicalization is by definition invariant to rotation and translation: \(X\) and \(R\!\cdot\!X + t\) are the same molecule in the same orbit, so any exact, numerically-stable canonicalization must map them to the identical representative, giving EMD \(=0\). Our method does exactly this — \(\approx 10^{-8}\), machine precision — on every rank and every point group tested. "No errors everywhere" is not evidence of some special robustness property; it simply confirms our implementation is a faithful, stable exact canonicalization, as it is supposed to be.

The finding is the comparison, not our zero. ASUN also claims to be an exact canonicalization, yet it does not score 0 even with zero coordinate noise — only a rigid rotation + translation of the input. It breaks its own claimed rotation-invariance on symmetric molecules: the rank-3 tail in Table 1, and systematic failures on point groups such as Ci and D3d observed in the underlying runs. The right framing is "ASUN fails its own consistency check; ours holds" — not "ours is noise-proof."

Honest caveat (not this page’s main result) Separately, under added coordinate noise, our method is no longer exactly 0 and destabilizes on genuine \(\ge3\)-fold axes — the same degeneracy dichotomy this project is built around. That is a real limitation of the discontinuous-canonicalization approach, but it is a different experiment from the zero-noise rigid-transform test above, which is the one both Table 1 and Table 2 report.

Robustness to inputs: ours never crashes on any molecule or point group tested. ASUN raises AssertionError on cubic / octahedral / bipyramidal shapes.

6. PCA frame, then the \(O(2)\times O(1)\) residual

Centre the cloud \(X\in\mathbb R^{n\times3}\), form the Gram matrix \(C=X^\top X\in\mathbb R^{3\times3}\), and diagonalize \(C=V\Lambda V^\top\) (eigenvalues ascending). Our stratum is exactly the one PCA leaves ambiguous: a \(\ge3\)-fold axis (C\(_{3h}\), C\(_{4h}\), C\(_{6h}\), D\(_{nh}\)) forces the spectrum \(\Lambda=(\lambda,\mu,\mu)\) or \((\mu,\mu,\lambda)\) — one double eigenvalue and one simple. The double eigenvalue’s eigenspace is a 2-plane, so its eigenbasis is fixed only up to an \(O(2)\) rotation/reflection; the simple eigenvector is fixed only up to a sign. Writing \(Y=XV\), the eigendecomposition is ambiguous exactly up to \[ Y \;\longmapsto\; Y\,Q,\qquad Q=\mathrm{blockdiag}(O,\varepsilon),\quad O\in O(2),\ \varepsilon\in\{+1,-1\}, \] a right action on the three columns of \(Y\): the residual group \(O(2)\times O(1)\). Canonicalizing \(Y\) against this residual (and sorting rows against \(S_n\)) yields an exact pose \(c(X)\) with \(c(gX)=c(X)\) for every \(g\in O(3)\times S_n\). PCA has done the heavy lifting — collapsing all of \(O(3)\) down to a finite-plus-one-circle residual; only \(O(2)\times O(1)\) is left to fix.

The one trap: never canonicalize the 2-plane alone It is tempting to fix the \(O(2)\) by canonicalizing the projection of \(Y\) onto the degenerate 2-plane with the 2D polar routine, then attach the axis column. This breaks invariance. The 2D shadow can carry more symmetry than the cloud: three coaxial \(k\)-rings staggered by \(\tfrac13\)-steps project to a regular \(3k\)-gon (apparent order \(3k\)) while the true axis symmetry is only \(C_k\). A frame fixed from the shadow discards the height that breaks the spurious symmetries. The fix: the 2D routine only proposes candidate in-plane orientations (one per non-axial point, each rotated onto the \(+y\) ray, times a reflection); the winner is the argmin of the quantized row key of the full \((y_1,y_2,y_3)\) triple, axis column included. Surviving ties are then genuine symmetries, hence harmless.

7. The axis sign is just another argmin dimension

The sign \(\varepsilon\) of the simple (axis) column needs no heuristic — no “orient so some reference atom has positive height,” which has nothing to grip on planar molecules where every atom sits at height zero. We simply enumerate \(\varepsilon\in\{+1,-1\}\) as one more axis of the argmin. Two outcomes, both correct:

  • The flip changes the cloud ⇒ the quantized keys differ and the argmin picks one deterministically.
  • Flipping the last column is itself a symmetry — some \(g\in H\) sends the axis \(w\mapsto-w\) (e.g. a planar C\(_{3h}\) whose horizontal mirror \(\sigma_h\) reverses the axis) ⇒ both signs produce the same output cloud, a harmless tie. Nothing to decide.

That is the whole story for the sign: when \(w\mapsto-w\) is a symmetry the two candidates coincide; otherwise the full-cloud key breaks the tie. No stability argument is needed — these are exact ties, not near-ties.

Grill the residual (1) Why is the residual exactly \(O(2)\times O(1)\)? Which orthogonal matrices commute with \(\Lambda=\mathrm{diag}(\mu,\mu,\lambda)\), and why does that pin down the block structure? (2) On the staggered three-ring cloud, how many candidate frames tie at the minimum, and which group elements do they correspond to? (3) For a planar C\(_{3h}\) molecule, write \(\Lambda\): which column is the simple one, and why is the \(\pm\) axis genuinely unfixable — yet harmless?

8. Verification

The construction is implemented in code/canonicalize_pca_degenerate.py, with the eigenframe exposed as a seam — canonicalize_in_eigenframe(X,λ,V) — so the residual invariance can be tested by feeding it \(V\) and \(V\,\mathrm{blockdiag}(O,\varepsilon)\) directly. The two invariances the method is supposed to satisfy — \(O(2)\times O(1)\) on the eigenvectors and \(O(3)\times S_n\) on the cloud — both hold to machine precision, and the single construction trap (canonicalizing the 2-plane in isolation) is fenced off by an explicit failing baseline in the tests.

Where this sits This is the linear-algebra route to the same orbit representative the WWV construction (Wolter–Woo–Volz 1985) reaches combinatorially: on the \(\ge3\)-fold stratum PCA already removes all of \(O(3)\) except \(O(2)\times O(1)\), and a 2D polar argmin plus a sign finishes the job. No eigenvalue degeneracy is ever divided by — we canonicalize the degenerate plane, never the (undefined) individual eigenvectors.
